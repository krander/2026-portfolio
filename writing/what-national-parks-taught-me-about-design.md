I've been to 22 national parks. I've thought about the wayfinding in all of them more than any reasonable person should.

This started when I was hiking in Zion and noticed that the trail signs never told you everything at once. At the trailhead, you got the big picture: the trail name, the distance, the difficulty rating, a small map. At each junction, you got what you needed for the next decision: left or right, how far to the next landmark, whether there was water ahead. You were never overwhelmed with information you didn't need yet. You were never left with a decision and no information at all.

That's progressive disclosure. And the Park Service has been doing it on laminated signs in the desert for decades.

## The part where I connect this to UX

The parallel is obvious, so I'll try to say something more specific than "both trails and apps should give you information at the right time."

What I actually think about is the confidence management. A good trail sign system makes you feel capable. You always know enough to take the next step. You don't need the full picture — you need enough certainty to keep moving. When you reach the next junction, the sign is there, confirming that you're on the right path, giving you the next piece.

A bad onboarding flow does the opposite. It either dumps everything on you at once (here are all 14 features, please configure them now) or it tells you almost nothing and asks you to trust that it'll make sense eventually. Neither of these things builds confidence. Both of them make you want to sit down on a rock and wonder if you should turn back.

## The ranger metaphor I keep coming back to

The best experience I've had in a national park — from a wayfinding perspective — was at a backcountry permit station in the North Cascades. The ranger spent about four minutes with me. She didn't recite the rulebook. She told me the three things that were actually going to affect my specific trip: where the snow was, which bridge was out, and that bears had been active near one of my planned campsites.

She had a complete picture of everything I might need to know. She gave me the three things that mattered for my situation. That's a very specific skill — knowing everything and then choosing carefully what to surface.

That's what I try to do in a research synthesis. Not: here are all 47 findings. But: here are the three things that change what we build.

## The thing that doesn't translate

Trails don't have error states. If you go the wrong way, the consequence is extra miles and maybe some embarrassment. You can't really crash a trail. The stakes are low enough that the system can afford to be minimal.

Products can't always do that. Sometimes you need to surface more information because the consequence of getting it wrong is data loss or a missed appointment or a medical error. The progressive disclosure principle still holds — you're still trying to give people what they need for the next decision — but what counts as "enough" is calibrated differently.

I think about that calibration a lot. The parks taught me the principle. Every project since has been about figuring out where the stakes actually are.
