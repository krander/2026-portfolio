I've been thinking about the ratio lately. How much time I spend watching, scrolling, listening to podcasts... absorbing other people's ideas... versus actually making something and putting it out there. The ratio is alarming.

That being said, I do think there's a version of consuming that feels productive. Learning, staying informed, getting inspired. But at some point it becomes a substitute for the thing itself.

So in a world where a lot of trash AI made content is being spewed out onto the internet, I'm going to try to shift the balance. Write more than I read. Publish more than I save. The bar doesn't need to be high or perfectly calibrated by AI. To quote Demi Lovato from Camp Rock, "This is me." For better or worse.

That said, I've also been more intentional about where I put things. Not everything belongs here, and not everything needs to go viral to be worthwhile. 

Yancey Strickler wrote about the idea of the internet as a dark forest back in 2019. His argument was that the open web had become so hostile (ads, tracking, trolling, algorithmic manipulation) that people were retreating into quieter, more private spaces: newsletters, group chats, invite-only communities. Places where you know who's in the room.

That framing stuck with me. The mainstream internet isn't neutral ground. Putting something out there publicly means it exists in a specific ecosystem... one that rewards provocation, optimizes for engagement, and doesn't always treat ideas charitably. That's worth being aware of.

But Strickler also noticed that retreating entirely from the public arena had a cost. To use his bowling alley metaphor: people show up to a bowling league be with each other, not because the venue is perfect. If everyone with a thoughtful perspective goes dark, public spaces on the internet don't improve... they just get louder and worse.

That is the tension that I keep coming back to. I'm writing for myself first. Don't disappear entirely. Show up somewhere people can find you... and that place is here.
